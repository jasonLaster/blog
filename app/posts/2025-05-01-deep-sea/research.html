Deep-Sea Mining in the 1970s vs. Today’s High-Tech Approach

In the 1970s, deep-sea mining was a wild new idea with very rudimentary technology. Companies launched experimental missions to scoop up polymetallic nodules (lumpy black mineral-rich rocks) from the seabed using crude methods. For example, in 1970 a company called Deepsea Ventures tested a “hydraulic airlift” – basically a giant vacuum pipe – to suck nodules up from 800 meters deepenvironmentandsociety.org. Several international consortia poured hundreds of millions of dollars into these early systems, racing to grab the “valuable metals just waiting to be scooped up” on the ocean floorenvironmentandsociety.org. There were essentially no environmental regulations or global oversight back then, and people gave little thought to the deep ocean ecosystem. In fact, one science team in 1970 was more interested in whether pumping nutrient-rich deep water to the surface might help ocean life (they imagined it could fertilize plankton blooms) rather than worrying about damage belowenvironmentandsociety.org. The 1970s mining gear was disruptive and imprecise – like dragging a heavy rake or dredge over the seabed, gathering everything in its path. It caused a lot of disturbance, and no one really measured the harm at the time (environmental impact studies were basically nonexistent).

Modern deep-sea mining looks very different. Take the example of The Metals Company (TMC) – a company today aiming to collect these same polymetallic nodules in a much more high-tech (and supposedly careful) way. TMC’s approach uses remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) that are like tank-like robots on caterpillar tracks, equipped with cameras and sensors. Detailed mapping and surveys are done beforehand, so operators know exactly where high densities of nodules are and can plan routes to avoid sensitive features. Instead of blindly plowing the seafloor, the ROV moves methodically, guided from a control room on a surface ship. The vehicle has suction heads on its underside – essentially an underwater vacuum – that gently slurp up the loose nodules lying on the sedimentpbs.org. Unlike the old days, this system is designed for selective collection: it targets the potato-sized nodules and tries to leave behind most of the sediment and non-nodule rocks. The collected nodules are funneled into a riser pipe – a long tube connecting the ROV to the ship – and pumped up to the surface as a slurry. Importantly, the modern machines separate a lot of the mud and sediment on the spot, so mostly just nodules (and some water) go up the pipe, while most sediment is discharged right back onto the seafloor behind the rovernews.mit.edu. All of this is done under permits and regulations from the International Seabed Authority (ISA) nowadays, with environmental monitoring requirements that were absent in the 1970s. In short: 1970s mining was like using a blunt shovel in the dark, whereas today’s is more like a Roomba under careful watch – high-tech, more controlled, and at least somewhat regulated, though still far from impact-free.

How Modern Deep-Sea Mining Works (Step by Step)

Modern deep-sea mining of polymetallic nodules follows a complex workflow. Here’s a step-by-step look at how companies like TMC carry out their operations today:

  1. Environmental Surveys & Mapping: Before any mining, scientists and engineers survey the targeted seafloor area. They use sonar mapping and robotic explorers to create detailed maps of the seabed terrain and nodule fields. Baseline biological studies are done – cataloguing species, habitats, and environmental conditions – to understand what lives there and set a reference point for later impactpbs.orgpbs.org. (For instance, recent surveys in the Pacific’s Clarion-Clipperton Zone revealed ~5,000 species, most new to science, living among the nodulespbs.org!)

  2. Planning the Mining Path: Using the maps, the company plans where the ROV collector will drive. They avoid areas with obstacles (like rocky outcrops or fragile coral gardens) and might designate “no-go” zones to protect special habitats. The goal is to maximize nodule collection while minimizing disturbance.

  3. Deploying the Collector ROV: From the deck of the mining vessel (often a converted ship, like TMC’s Hidden Gem), the heavy ROV is lowered into the ocean on a cable. This robot is about the size of a large tractor – one prototype (GSR’s “Patania II”) was 3 m tall and 4 m wide, about one-third the size of future commercial vehiclesnews.mit.edu. The ROV is connected to the ship by a power/data umbilical and a riser pipe for slurry transport.

  4. Collecting Nodules on the Seafloor: The ROV drives along the seabed following the pre-planned route. It has cameras and lights to navigate the pitch-dark depths (~4,000-5,000 m down). As it moves, it lowers its suction head to the sediment, vacuuming up nodules lying on or slightly buried in the mudpbs.org. The design tries to minimize how much sediment is sucked in – usually the nodules are separated from mud inside the machine. In fact, inside the ROV, a sorter separates out most of the sediment from the nodules; the nodules go into a hopper and then into the riser pipe, while a lot of the muddy sediment is expelled back out behind the machine on the seafloornews.mit.edu. This way, they reduce clogging and also keep the worst sediment plume near the bottom.

  5. Lifting to the Surface: A powerful pump system (sometimes a continuous airlift or hydraulic pump) drives the slurry of nodules and seawater up the riser pipe to the surface vessel. This pipe can be 4 kilometers long in abyssal sitesoceannews.com, so engineering it is a feat. In tests, TMC and its partner Allseas successfully connected a prototype collector to a 700m pipe section, and plan to extend to full depthoceannews.com. The nodules are essentially “vacuumed” straight up to the ship.

  6. Onboard Processing: Once on the ship, the mixture goes through a dewatering system. The nodules are sieved out from the water and remaining silt. The collected nodules (hard rock pieces) are stored on the vessel’s deck. The excess water and fine sediment – now separated – are often pumped back down into the ocean at depth. (The idea is to return this water below the surface layer, to avoid creating plumes in shallow waters. However, exactly how and where to discharge is an ongoing debate.)

  7. Transportation and Refining: The vessel periodically transfers the harvested nodules to transport ships or brings them to port. The nodules then go to processing plants on land, where metals like nickel, cobalt, manganese, and copper are extracted for use in batteries and alloys. This is similar to refining ore from a land-based mine, except these “ore rocks” are from the seafloor.

  8. Monitoring and Mitigation: Throughout the operation, environmental monitoring is conducted. The mining ship or accompanying research vessels use sensors and ROVs to monitor the sediment plumes, noise, and water chemistry around the operation. They also observe how marine life is responding (if fish flee the area, etc.). The data is used to adapt the process and will inform regulators. Once a mining block is finished, the company may be required to allow a recovery period and later assess how the site is recolonizing. In pilot trials, independent scientists have followed behind the machines to measure how much sediment was stirred up and how far it spreadnews.mit.edunews.mit.edu.

This modern workflow is much more fine-tuned than the 1970s efforts. But even with all this care and tech, the fundamental action is the same: scraping the ocean floor to collect resources, which inevitably causes disturbance.

An Ocean Floor Carpeted with Ancient Treasures (and Life)

Above: A vast deep-sea plain ~4,000 m down in the Pacific, covered in dark polymetallic nodules. These potato-sized nodules formed over millions of years and contain metals like manganese, nickel, and cobalt. They also serve as hard habitat for many deep-sea creatures. (Image: NOAA Ocean Exploration)

The appeal of deep-sea mining comes from scenes like the one above – a seemingly endless carpet of metal-rich nodules just sitting on the seafloor. These nodules are truly ancient: they grow at an incredibly slow rate (about 10 million years to form a nodule the size of a golf ball, by one estimatepbs.org!). They precipitate metals from seawater over geological time, often starting on little “seeds” like a shark tooth or shell fragmentpbs.org. In places like the Clarion-Clipperton Zone (CCZ) in the Pacific, there are more nickel and cobalt in these fields of nodules than in all known land depositspbs.org. And unlike land ores, you don’t have to dig a mine shaft or blast a mountainside – they’re right there on the seabed surface.

However, what these nodules are sitting on is not just barren mud. The deep-sea floor is a living environment, albeit a sparse and slow-paced one. Biodiversity in nodule areas is surprisingly high: one study found roughly 90% of the species collected in the CCZ were new to science and unique to that regionpbs.org. Many creatures make use of the nodules themselves. For example, sponges and corals attach to the hard nodule surfaces (hard substrate is rare on the soft abyssal plain). Certain “Casper” octopuses (so-called for their ghostly white appearance) have been observed laying their eggs on those sponge stalks attached to nodulespbs.org – meaning the nodules indirectly provide nursery sites for octopus babies! Brittle stars, worms, and microscopic “water bears” live among the nodules toopbs.org. So, when a mining robot sweeps up nodules, it’s also removing habitat and potentially a whole web of life that depended on those rocks.

The deep sea ecosystem operates on ”slow time”. Nutrients are scarce, temperatures low, and creatures often grow and reproduce very slowly. A famous example is the deep-sea octopus mother that scientists nicknamed “Octomom.” In a 2014 study, researchers observed a mother octopus (Graneledone boreopacifica) in the Monterey Canyon who brooded her eggs for 53 months (4.5 years) – by far the longest known egg-brooding period of any animalearthsky.orgearthsky.org. She clung to the rock face guarding her 160 or so eggs, never leaving to eat, slowly starving but keeping her eggs safe until they finally hatchedearthsky.orgearthsky.org. This incredible story (featured in a Radiolab episode) highlights how delicate and long-lived deep-sea life can be. Many deep creatures live life in the slow lane, whether it’s octopuses brooding for years, corals that live for millennia, or fish that take decades to mature. This means that if their habitat is disturbed or destroyed, recovery might take decades or longer – if it happens at all.

Environmental Concerns: Plumes, Life Loss, and Carbon Release

Given the above, it’s no surprise that deep-sea mining raises serious environmental concerns. Here are the main issues scientists and environmentalists worry about:

  • Seafloor Habitat Destruction: The most direct impact is the removal of nodules and the top layer of the seabed over large areas. The mining tracks essentially plow the seafloor, killing or displacing whatever can’t move out of the way. Habitats that took millions of years to develop (like those nodule fields with their attached life) are stripped bare in minutes. And because nodules do not regenerate on any human timescale, that habitat is effectively gone permanentlypbs.orgpbs.org. Even 26 years after a small test mining disturbance in 1989, scientists found the plow tracks still clearly visible on the seaflooreandt.theiet.org. In that area, the normal carbon cycling activity of the ecosystem was still reduced by one-third, indicating the food web had not recovered to its prior state decades latereandt.theiet.org.

  • Sediment Plumes: Both at the seafloor and from the returned water, mining creates clouds of fine sediment that can spread and drift. On the seafloor, as the ROV scoops nodules, it stirs up mud in its wake, creating a turbidity plume. Recent research by MIT and others found that the collector vehicle produces a dense, murky cloud that mostly stays within ~2 meters of the bottom initiallynews.mit.edu. Most of that sediment resettles nearby, but currents can later pick up the smallest particles and carry them furthernews.mit.edu. Additionally, when the ship pumps the filtered water (from dewatering) back into the ocean, if released at mid-water depths, it could form a second plume in the water column. These plumes can smother marine life by blanketing animals and clogging filter-feeding organisms (like sponges or some jellyfish) with silt. There is also concern for mid-water creatures: one laboratory study showed that deep-sea jellyfish experience stress and metabolic harm when exposed to sediment concentrations like those expected from mining plumesnature.com. Essentially, a plume could choke creatures not just on the seabed but also those drifting in the dark ocean depths.

  • Noise, Light, and Disturbance: The deep ocean is a realm of quiet and darkness. Mining operations will introduce loud noise from machinery and pumps, as well as bright lights on ROVs and surface vessels. Whales and other marine mammals generally don’t dive to 5000 m, but noise can travel, and there could be impacts on species in the mid-depths (for example, disruption of whale navigation or fish behavior). The light and noise could scare off or disorient deep-sea fish and invertebrates that live in perpetual darkness.

  • Biodiversity Loss and Extinction: Because so many deep-sea species are localized (found only in certain areas) and slow to reproduce, there’s a real risk that mining a region could wipe out species before we even know them. Scientists worry about “biological unknowns” – there could be animals specialized to live on those nodules or in that sediment that won’t survive elsewhere. If we drive them to extinction, we lose unique parts of Earth’s biodiversity. A recent Nature study underscored this by showing that a site in the Pacific where mining was tested in 1979 still had reduced species diversity 44 years later compared to undisturbed areashw.ac.uk. Recovery is painfully slow, if it happens at all, in the deep sea.

  • Ecosystem and Carbon Cycle Impacts: The deep sea plays a role in the global carbon cycle – it’s a sink for organic carbon (like marine snow that falls from above and gets buried in sediment). Disrupting the seafloor could resuspend carbon that had been locked away, sending it back into circulation. One analysis suggested that for each square kilometer of seabed mined, roughly 190 tons of stored carbon could be released through stirred-up sediment, potentially entering the water as CO₂ over timeecowatch.com. In effect, deep-sea mining might inadvertently burb up carbon that contributes to climate change, counteracting some benefits of obtaining metals for clean energy tech. Additionally, the disturbance can alter microbial communities that drive nutrient and carbon cycling; as noted, decades-long disruption to the microbial food web was observed after the 1989 DISCOL seafloor disturbance experimenteandt.theiet.org.

In summary, the deep ocean is not a desolate wasteland – it’s an interconnected environment where damage can have cascading effects. The sediment plumes, habitat removal, and slow recovery rates all raise red flags that this new industry could inflict harm that lasts well beyond our lifetimes.

Objections and Concerns from Scientists and Environmental Groups

All these potential impacts have led to significant pushback against deep-sea mining from scientists, conservation organizations, and even some governments. Marine biologists and oceanographers repeatedly emphasize that we still know very little about deep-sea ecosystems – and that caution is warranted. In fact, the consensus among many experts is that “we don’t know enough to proceed” with mining, and thus we probably shouldn’t proceed (at least not yet)pbs.org. In 2021, over 600 scientists signed an open letter calling for a moratorium on deep-sea mining until more research is done. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) and groups like WWF have echoed these calls, essentially urging a “pause” before humanity rushes to industrialize the ocean’s depths.

Environmental NGOs argue that deep-sea mining could cause irreversible damage to a realm that until now has been relatively untouched. They point out that once species or habitats are gone, you can’t replace them – no one is going to “replant” a slow-growing sponge garden 4 km underwater. Moreover, there’s an ethical argument: the deep sea is considered the common heritage of mankind under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, so its exploitation should be extremely carefully managed, if allowed at all. “We have to stop a new environmental catastrophe before it begins,” said one member of the European Parliament in 2023, warning that if reckless deep-sea mining launches, it could go down in history as “the destruction of the deep sea”sciencenorway.no. A Nature editorial likewise criticized countries moving forward hastily, stating that a nation pushing seabed mining undermines global ocean protection effortssciencenorway.nosciencenorway.no.

Specific objections raised include: the lack of comprehensive long-term impact studies, the difficulty in monitoring such remote operations, and the question of whether we truly need these minerals from the sea or could obtain them through better recycling and land-based mining improvements. There’s also concern about the governance by the ISA – a relatively small, Jamaica-based body that some fear might be influenced by industry interests and may not effectively enforce environmental safeguards on the high seas. In July 2023, the ISA faced intense pressure at its meeting, and Nature reported the looming decision on mining as “how bad is it?” with emerging data hinting at long-term damage even from small-scale testsnature.com. In November 2023, a study (the first of its kind) demonstrated jellyfish being harmed by mining-like sediment, reinforcing these worriesnature.com.

On the other side, the mining companies (and the nations sponsoring them) argue that deep-sea mining can be done responsibly and that the metals are essential for green technologies (batteries for electric cars, etc.). The Metals Company, for instance, often claims that obtaining battery metals from nodules will do less overall harm than traditional mining on land (which can involve deforestation, child labor, toxic tailings, etc.). They also highlight that no communities live in the deep ocean, whereas land mines often impact local communities and Indigenous peoples. This has led to a contentious debate: a choice between potential deep-sea ecosystem damage and the demands of the clean energy transition. Environmental groups are essentially saying, “Not so fast – we need to make sure we’re not solving one problem by creating another.” As of now (2025), no commercial deep-sea mining has begun in international waters, partly due to this pressure delaying regulations. The coming years will be critical in determining if and how this industry moves forward.

Deep-Sea Mining vs. Deep-Sea Drilling: Different Risks in the Deep Ocean

It’s worth comparing deep-sea mining to another well-known seafloor activity: deep-sea oil drilling. Both involve heavy industrial equipment operating in the ocean’s depths, but their risk profiles and impacts differ:

  • Catastrophic Spill vs. Chronic Disturbance: The biggest fear with deep-sea drilling (for oil) is a blowout or spill – as tragically seen in the 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster, where ~4 million barrels of oil gushed into the Gulf of Mexico over 87 daysepa.gov. That kind of event can spread pollution over huge areas, coating shorelines and killing marine life en masse. Deep-sea mining doesn’t carry a risk of oil spills, since it’s not dealing with hydrocarbons under pressure. In that sense, mining is less likely to cause a sudden large-scale catastrophe. However, mining causes a more distributed, long-term impact: scraping hundreds of square kilometers of seafloor and creating sediment plumes. It’s a widespread habitat destruction that, while not as acutely dramatic as an oil spill, could have long-lasting effects concentrated in the deep ocean. Drilling accidents are rare but disastrous; mining damage is certain and continuous over the operation area, even if no single event is “disastrous” in the spill sense.

  • Pollution: Oil drilling (even without spills) can introduce drilling muds, chemicals, and small oil leaks into the environment, and if a well strikes oil, a failure means toxic crude release. Mining will introduce noise and possibly some heavy metal disturbance, but it’s mostly a physical impact (rocks and mud) rather than chemical pollution. There is concern that mining could release toxic elements from sediments (for example, if deep sediments contain mercury or other elements that get re-suspended), but this is less understood. In general, oil is poisonous to many forms of life, whereas seafloor sediment is not toxic per se – it’s the blanketing effect that is harmful.

  • Affected Areas: Drilling impacts tend to be localized around the drill site (barring a spill). A single well has a footprint of maybe tens of meters, plus some area affected by discharged drill cuttings. Mining, by design, will impact hundreds to thousands of square kilometers if an operation runs for years. For example, a commercial nodule mining operation might plan to collect millions of tons of nodules, meaning essentially raking clean vast swaths of seabed. So the spatial scale of routine impact is much larger for mining. Drilling’s worst-case scenario (a spill) can spread damage far and wide including surface waters and coasts, whereas mining’s damage is mostly in the deep and doesn’t directly touch the surface or coasts.

  • Recovery and Visibility: After an oil well is drilled and finished (assuming no accident), the seafloor might recover relatively quickly – a few years for the benthic life to re-establish around the abandoned wellhead, since the physical structure is still there (and often becomes an artificial reef). But after mining, the seabed is stripped of the structures (nodules) and many animals. As we noted, recovery could be extremely slow in the deep sea, potentially centuries or never to regain original conditionshw.ac.uk. Also, oil spills are very visible (images of oil-soaked birds, etc., galvanize public outcry), whereas damage a few kilometers under the ocean is out of sight and harder for the public to grasp. This difference means there’s a risk that mining impacts could be underappreciated simply because they’re not readily seen – until scientists go down to look years later and find an eerie underwater desert.

In summary, deep-sea drilling is like a high-risk, high-consequence gamble (most days okay, one day very bad), while deep-sea mining is a steady pressure with guaranteed disruption but perhaps no single “oh no” moment. Both industries need strong regulation: drilling taught us through disasters like Deepwater Horizon how bad things can get without proper safety, and mining is now teaching us (through test results) how significant the cumulative effects might be on ocean health.

Conclusion: Balancing Curiosity, Need, and Caution

The story of deep-sea mining, from the freewheeling experiments of the 1970s to the sophisticated robot miners of today, is a testament to human ingenuity – and a reminder of our responsibility. The deep ocean is Earth’s last frontier, a place of alien creatures and ancient resources, slow-growing corals and brooding octopus mothers. It took billions of years for those ecosystems to evolve and millions of years to accumulate those mineral treasures on the seabed. The question we face now is: how do we balance our need for crucial metals with the need to protect our planet’s most untouched ecosystems?

For now, the world watches as companies like The Metals Company conduct their pilot runs, and as regulatory bodies debate rules that could launch a new industry. It’s a classic scenario of exploration vs. preservation. Standing in 2025, we have more knowledge than those 1970s pioneers – we know there’s delicate life down there and that our actions have consequences – but we are also more desperate for resources to fuel a green economy. Families discussing this around the dinner table might wonder if the phones in our pockets or the electric car in the driveway will one day contain metals vacuumed from the abyssal plains. It’s an exciting and unsettling thought.

One thing is certain: any decision to begin deep-sea mining on a large scale should be made with great caution and full awareness of what’s at stake. As one Nature article headline aptly asked, when contemplating opening this Pandora’s box: “Deep-sea mining could soon be approved — how bad is it?”nature.com. The coming years will likely give us that answer. In the meantime, scientists continue to study the deep sea’s secrets – from sediment plumes to octopus brooding – hoping to guide us toward choices that won’t leave the ocean’s cradle of life irreversibly disrupted.
