Tufts Graduate Student Detained (Rumeysa Öztürk)

On March 25, 2025, ICE agents surrounded and arrested Rumeysa Öztürk, a 30-year-old Turkish doctoral student at Tufts University, as she walked to break her Ramadan fast near her Somerville apartment. Authorities invoked a little-used “foreign policy” provision to terminate her F-1 visa, alleging her co-authorship of a 2024 op-ed calling for Tufts to divest from companies linked to Israel amounted to support for Hamas. She was flown to a detention center in Basile, Louisiana, without prior notice to her lawyers or the court. Öztürk’s legal team successfully persuaded the U.S. 2nd Circuit to pause her transfer back to New England while they contest the constitutionality of her cross-country detention, arguing it violates her rights to free speech and due process. Protests at Tufts and a wave of congressional letters (led by Reps. Pressley, Warren, and Markey) have spotlighted her case as emblematic of a broader crackdown on campus dissent under Trump’s second administration. AP NewsPBS: Public Broadcasting Service

Gay Venezuelan Makeup Artist/Hairdresser Deported (Andry José Hernández Romero)

On April 1, 2025, Andry José Hernández Romero, a 31-year-old Venezuelan makeup artist and hairdresser, was secretly deported to El Salvador’s Terrorism Confinement Center (CECOT) under the Alien Enemies Act, a statute last invoked in World War II. U.S. officials claimed his traditional “Three Kings” crown tattoos signified membership in the Tren de Aragua gang, despite widespread cultural use of such tattoos in his hometown. Lawyers say Hernández Romero had a credible fear finding for his asylum claim and presented no evidence of wrongdoing. His abrupt removal without a hearing has sparked legal challenges and congressional inquiries, with Rep. Robert Garcia demanding documentation on ICE’s decision and the evidence used to brand him a “gang member.” CBS NewsThe Guardian

Harvard Researcher Detained Over Frog Samples (Kseniia Petrova)

Kseniia Petrova, a 30-year-old Russian biomedical researcher at Harvard Medical School, was detained February 16, 2025, at Boston’s Logan Airport after carrying noninfectious frog embryo samples back from France without formally declaring them to CBP. Rather than issue the typical fine for a customs paperwork error, CBP revoked her J-1 visa on the spot. She was transferred to ICE custody in Vermont and then to a detention facility in Richwood, Louisiana. Federal officials accuse her of “smuggling biological substances,” though her visa cancellation and looming deportation have drawn criticism from judges, who recently deemed ICE’s deportation papers “legally deficient,” and from Harvard colleagues warning her removal jeopardizes vital cancer research. An immigration judge has ordered ICE to bolster its evidence within a week. AP NewsPBS: Public Broadcasting Service

Wife of Coast Guard Member Arrested On-Base

On April 24, 2025, the spouse of an active-duty U.S. Coast Guardsman was arrested by Homeland Security Investigations at Naval Air Station Key West during a routine security check for base housing access. ICE records show her work visa had expired in 2017, and she was subject to a lawful removal order issued thereafter. The Coast Guard confirmed cooperation with the arrest, which left her husband—assigned to the USCGC Mohawk—“shocked” and unable to prevent her transfer to ICE custody. The incident underscores that “sensitive location” protections no longer shield military bases from immigration enforcement under the current administration. AP NewsTask & Purpose

Mother & 4-Year-Old Child Deported (Cancer Case)

On April 25, 2025, ICE deported a Honduran mother alongside her two U.S. citizen children from New Orleans—among them a 4-year-old boy suffering from metastatic cancer—within 24 hours of their check-in. According to the ACLU and National Immigration Project, the mother was given no meaningful chance to transfer custody of her children to U.S. caregivers or consult an attorney; ICE relied on a hastily produced handwritten note as “proof” the mother chose family removal. Federal Judge Terry Doughty has slated a May 16 hearing “to dispel strong suspicion” that a U.S. citizen was deported without due process. Civil-rights advocates decry the action as both a violation of children’s rights and a return to family-separation tactics. AP NewsPBS: Public Broadcasting Service
